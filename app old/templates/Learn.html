Portfolio Theory

Modern Portfolio Theory

Harry Markowitz's 1952 paper changed investing forever. The core insight: what matters is not the risk of individual securities, but how they behave together as a portfolio.

The Key Insight

Before Markowitz, investors evaluated stocks one at a time — is this stock risky? Is it a good investment? Markowitz showed that this was fundamentally the wrong question. What matters is how each security contributes to the portfolio's overall risk and return.

The revolutionary idea: two stocks can each be volatile individually, but if they tend to move in opposite directions, combining them produces a portfolio that is less volatile than either alone. This is the mathematical foundation of diversification.

Key Concept

You can reduce portfolio risk without reducing expected return by combining assets whose returns are not perfectly correlated. This is the only "free lunch" in finance.

The Efficient Frontier

For any given set of assets, Markowitz proved there is a set of "efficient" portfolios — those that offer the maximum expected return for a given level of risk. Plotting all efficient portfolios produces a curve called the efficient frontier.

Any portfolio below the efficient frontier is suboptimal: you are taking more risk than necessary for the return you are getting. Any portfolio on the frontier is mathematically optimal given your assets.

In FlyingFunds, the allocation chart shows your current portfolio weights. A future feature will plot your portfolio on the efficient frontier so you can see exactly how close to optimal your allocation is.

Portfolio Variance Formula
σ²_p = Σᵢ Σⱼ wᵢ wⱼ σᵢⱼ

Where:
• wᵢ, wⱼ = weights of assets i and j
• σᵢⱼ = covariance between assets i and j
• The double sum runs over all pairs of assets
Worked Example

Suppose you hold two stocks: AAPL (40% weight, 25% annual volatility) and JPM (60% weight, 20% annual volatility) with a correlation of 0.3. The portfolio volatility is approximately 18.4% — lower than either stock alone. If they had zero correlation, it would be even lower (~15%). If perfectly correlated, it would be the weighted average (22%).

CAPM & Beta

The Capital Asset Pricing Model tells you how much return you should expect for the risk you're taking — and why diversification makes some risks "free" to eliminate.

What CAPM Says

Sharpe (1964) and Lintner (1965) built on Markowitz's work to derive a model of expected returns. The key insight: if investors hold well-diversified portfolios (as Markowitz suggested), the only risk that deserves compensation is the risk that cannot be diversified away — systematic risk, measured by beta (β).

Idiosyncratic risk — the risk specific to one company (a bad earnings report, a CEO scandal) — can be eliminated for free through diversification. Therefore, the market should not pay you extra return for bearing it.

The CAPM Formula
E(Rᵢ) = Rf + βᵢ × (E(Rm) − Rf)

Where:
• E(Rᵢ) = expected return of asset i
• Rf = risk-free rate (e.g. UK gilt yield)
• βᵢ = beta of the asset vs the market
• E(Rm) − Rf = market risk premium (~5-6% historically)
What Beta Means

Beta = 1.0 means the stock moves in line with the market. Beta = 1.5 means it amplifies market moves by 50% (more volatile). Beta = 0.5 means it is half as sensitive to market moves. Beta = −0.3 means it tends to move against the market (rare, and useful for hedging).

Example

If the risk-free rate is 4.5%, the market risk premium is 5.5%, and a stock has a beta of 1.2: CAPM predicts an expected return of 4.5% + 1.2 × 5.5% = 11.1% per year. If the stock has historically returned 15%, it appears to generate "alpha" — return above what CAPM predicts for its level of risk.

Limitations of CAPM

CAPM assumes investors hold the market portfolio, that returns are normally distributed, and that beta is stable over time. None of these hold perfectly in practice. The Fama-French three-factor model (adding size and value factors) and the Carhart four-factor model (adding momentum) extend CAPM to better explain observed returns.

The Sharpe Ratio

The Sharpe ratio is the single most widely used measure of risk-adjusted performance. It answers: how much return am I getting for each unit of risk I'm taking?

Sharpe Ratio Formula
S = (Rp − Rf) / σp

Where:
• Rp = portfolio return (annualised)
• Rf = risk-free rate (often 0 for simplicity)
• σp = portfolio standard deviation (annualised volatility)

How to Interpret Your Sharpe Ratio

  • Below 0: You are underperforming the risk-free rate. Your risk is not being rewarded at all.
  • 0 – 0.5: Inadequate return for the risk taken. Consider whether a passive index fund would serve you better.
  • 0.5 – 1.0: Acceptable. You are being compensated for your risk, though not exceptionally well.
  • 1.0 – 2.0: Good. Most professional fund managers operate in this range.
  • Above 2.0: Excellent — or suspiciously good. Very high Sharpe ratios often indicate unusual strategies, look-back bias, or the strategy has not yet experienced a stress event.
Important Limitation

The Sharpe ratio treats upside and downside volatility equally — a stock that occasionally surges upward looks "risky" even if it never crashes. The Sortino ratio addresses this by using only downside standard deviation in the denominator. It is a better measure for asymmetric return distributions.

FlyingFunds Context

Your Sharpe ratio is computed on the Analytics page using your portfolio's daily return series. A Sharpe of 0.8 means: for every 1% of annualised volatility you are bearing, you are earning 0.8% of excess return. Improving diversification (reducing correlation between holdings) reduces the denominator σp, which raises your Sharpe without necessarily increasing returns.

Risk Metrics

Value at Risk & CVaR

VaR answers the question every investor needs to ask: how bad could my losses get? CVaR goes further and tells you what to expect when things go really wrong.

What VaR Tells You

Value at Risk at 95% confidence over a one-day horizon means: "On 95 out of 100 days, my losses will not exceed this number." Equivalently: on 5 out of 100 days, my losses will be worse than VaR.

FlyingFunds computes Historical VaR — simply the 5th percentile of your portfolio's daily return distribution. This makes no assumption about the distribution of returns and directly reflects what your portfolio has actually done historically (or what the GBM simulation suggests).

Historical VaR (95%)
VaR₀.₉₅ = −Q₀.₀₅(R)

Where Q₀.₀₅(R) is the 5th percentile of the daily return distribution R.
CVaR₀.₉₅ = −E[R | R ≤ Q₀.₀₅(R)]
(The average return on the worst 5% of days)
CVaR: The Better Risk Measure

VaR has a famous flaw: it says nothing about how bad losses can get once you breach the threshold. During the 2008 crisis, banks discovered their VaR models were technically correct — losses exceeded VaR 5% of the time as expected — but the losses on those bad days were catastrophic. CVaR (Expected Shortfall) directly measures the average severity of those tail events.

Reading Your Metrics

If your portfolio shows VaR (95%) = −1.8% and CVaR (95%) = −2.6%, this means: on a typical bad day (worst 5%), you would expect to lose about 1.8% of your portfolio value. On truly extreme days (the worst of the worst), the average loss is 2.6%. For a £10,000 portfolio, that is £180 and £260 respectively on a single day.

Behavioural Finance

Behavioural Finance

Classical finance assumes investors are rational. Behavioural finance proves they are not — and documents the systematic, predictable ways that human psychology drives markets away from efficiency.

Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)

In one of the most influential papers in economics, Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky showed that people do not evaluate outcomes in absolute terms — they evaluate them relative to a reference point, and they are loss-averse: losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.

This explains many observed investor behaviours: holding losing stocks too long (hoping to "break even"), selling winners too early (locking in the pleasure of a gain), and feeling much worse about a 10% portfolio loss than happy about a 10% gain.

The Disposition Effect

Investors tend to sell winners too early and hold losers too long — the opposite of "cut your losses, let your winners run." This is loss aversion in action: realising a loss feels painful, so we avoid it by holding the position and hoping it recovers. The Investment Diary in FlyingFunds was designed to help you track this pattern in your own behaviour.

Why This Creates Market Anomalies

If investors systematically make the same mistakes, prices will deviate from fundamental value in predictable ways. Shleifer's "Inefficient Markets" (2000) shows that limits to arbitrage — the costs and risks of correcting mispricings — allow these deviations to persist. This is why momentum, value, and size effects survive even after they become widely known.

Markets

Efficient Markets Hypothesis

The EMH is one of the most debated ideas in finance: the claim that market prices already reflect all available information, making it impossible to consistently beat the market.

The Three Forms

  • Weak form: Prices reflect all historical price and volume data. Technical analysis (chart patterns) cannot generate consistent alpha.
  • Semi-strong form: Prices reflect all publicly available information — including earnings announcements, news, analyst reports. Fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market.
  • Strong form: Prices reflect all information, including private (insider) information. This form is generally rejected — insider trading is illegal precisely because it works.
What the Evidence Says

The evidence broadly supports weak and semi-strong efficiency for large-cap stocks in developed markets. Most active fund managers underperform their benchmark net of fees over the long run. Yet numerous anomalies (momentum, value premium, size effect) have been documented and persist — which either means markets are not fully efficient, or these anomalies represent compensation for risk that standard models do not capture.

Risk Metrics

Max Drawdown

Max drawdown measures the worst peak-to-trough decline in your portfolio's history. It is one of the most viscerally understandable risk measures.

What It Measures

Max drawdown = (Peak portfolio value − Trough portfolio value) / Peak portfolio value. A drawdown of −30% means your portfolio fell 30% from its high point before recovering. This is what you would have experienced if you had invested at the worst possible time in that period.

Why It Matters

Many investors can tolerate volatility on paper but panic-sell during a large drawdown, locking in permanent losses. Knowing your historical max drawdown helps you calibrate whether you can psychologically tolerate the strategy you are running. If your max drawdown is 40%, ask yourself: could I have held on through that without selling?
