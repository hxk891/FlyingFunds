Modern Portfolio Theory

Harry Markowitz's 1952 paper changed investing forever. The core insight: what matters is not the risk of individual securities, but how they behave together as a portfolio.

The Key Insight

Before Markowitz, investors evaluated stocks one at a time... is this stock risky? Markowitz showed that was fundamentally the wrong approach, and the wrong question. What matters is how each security contributes to the portfolio's overall risk and return.

Two stocks can each be volatile individually, however if they tend to move in opposite directions, combining them produces a portfolio that is less volatile than either alone, hence leading to the mathematical foundation of diversification.

The Free Lunch

You can reduce portfolio risk without reducing expected return, this can be done by combining assets whose returns are not perfectly correlated. This is the only "free lunch" in finance.. risk reduction at no cost.

The Efficient Frontier

Markowitz proved there is a set of "efficient" portfolios, those that offer maximum expected return for a given level of risk. Plotting all efficient portfolios produces a curve called the efficient frontier.

Any portfolio below the frontier is suboptimal: you're taking more risk than necessary for the return you're getting. In FlyingFunds, the Analytics Lab plots your portfolio against the efficient frontier so you can see exactly how optimal your allocation is.

Portfolio Variance Formula
σ²_p = Σᵢ Σⱼ wᵢ wⱼ σᵢⱼ

Where:
 • wᵢ, wⱼ = weights of assets i and j
 • σᵢⱼ = covariance between assets i and j
Worked Example

AAPL (40%, 25% vol) and JPM (60%, 20% vol) with correlation 0.3 → portfolio volatility ≈ 18.4%, lower than either stock alone. At zero correlation: ~15%. At perfect correlation: 22%.

CAPM & Beta

The Capital Asset Pricing Model links expected return to systematic risk, and explains why diversification lets investors eliminate some risks for free.

What CAPM Says

Built on Markowitz's work, CAPM (Sharpe 1964) shows that if investors hold well-diversified portfolios, the only risk that deserves compensation is systematic risk, meaning market-wide risk that cannot be diversified away, measured by beta (β).

Idiosyncratic risk, company-specific events like a CEO scandal, can be eliminated for free through diversification. The market should not pay you extra return for bearing it.

The CAPM Formula
E(Rᵢ) = Rf + βᵢ × (E(Rm) − Rf)

 • Rf = risk-free rate (e.g. gilt yield)
 • βᵢ = beta of the asset vs the market
 • E(Rm) − Rf = market risk premium (~5–6%)
What Beta Means

β = 1.0 → moves with the market. β = 1.5 → amplifies market moves 50%. β = 0.5 → half as sensitive. β < 0 → moves against the market (useful for hedging). Most tech stocks: β > 1.2. Utilities: β ≈ 0.4–0.7.

Example

Rf = 4.5%, market premium = 5.5%, β = 1.2 → CAPM predicts 11.1% return. If the stock returns 15%, it generates "alpha", excess return above what its risk level should deliver.

Limitations

CAPM assumes stable beta and normal return distributions, though neither holds perfectly. The Fama-French three-factor model (adding size and value) and Carhart four-factor model (adding momentum) better explain observed returns.

Diversification

The only free lunch in finance: the ability to reduce risk without sacrificing expected return, simply by combining assets wisely.

The Mathematics

When you combine two assets, portfolio variance depends on how their returns are correlated. If correlation is less than 1 (almost always), portfolio variance is lower than the weighted average of individual variances. Lower correlation → greater risk reduction.

Holding a mix of stocks, bonds, and geographies that don't all crash simultaneously cushions the overall impact when any one position falls.

Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk

Unsystematic risk (company-specific: earnings miss, CEO scandal) can be diversified away. Systematic risk (recessions, rate shocks) affects all assets and cannot. You can only eliminate unsystematic risk through diversification.

The Sharpe Ratio

The most widely used measure of risk-adjusted performance. How much return are you getting per unit of risk?

Sharpe Ratio Formula
S = (Rp − Rf) / σp

 • Rp = portfolio return (annualised)
 • Rf = risk-free rate
 • σp = portfolio standard deviation (annualised vol)

How to Interpret Your Score

  • Below 0: Underperforming the risk-free rate. Risk isn't being rewarded at all.
  • 0 – 0.5: Poor. A passive index fund may serve you better.
  • 0.5 – 1.0: Acceptable. You're being compensated for risk, though not exceptionally.
  • 1.0 – 2.0: Good. Most professional fund managers operate here.
  • Above 2.0: Excellent, or suspicious. Look for look-back bias.
~0.5S&P 500 long-run average
1.0+Professional target
2.0+Exceptional / rare
Important Limitation

The Sharpe ratio treats upside and downside volatility equally. A stock that occasionally surges looks "risky." The Sortino ratio uses only downside standard deviation, making it better for asymmetric return distributions.

FlyingFunds Context

A Sharpe of 0.8 means: for every 1% of annualised volatility you bear, you earn 0.8% of excess return. Improving diversification reduces σp, raising your Sharpe without necessarily increasing returns.

Value at Risk & CVaR

VaR answers the question every investor needs to ask: how bad could my losses get? CVaR tells you what to expect when things go really wrong.

What VaR Tells You

VaR at 95% confidence over one day means: "On 95 out of 100 days, my losses won't exceed this number." On 5 out of 100 days, losses will be worse. FlyingFunds computes Historical VaR, specifically the 5th percentile of your actual daily return distribution.

Historical VaR & CVaR (95%)
VaR₀.₉₅ = −Q₀.₀₅(R)
CVaR₀.₉₅ = −E[R | R ≤ Q₀.₀₅(R)]

CVaR = average loss on the worst 5% of days
CVaR: The Better Risk Measure

VaR says nothing about how bad losses get once you breach the threshold. In 2008, banks' VaR models were technically correct, but losses on bad days were catastrophic. CVaR (Expected Shortfall) directly measures the average severity of tail events.

Reading Your Metrics

VaR (95%) = −1.8%, CVaR (95%) = −2.6% means: on a typical bad day (worst 5%) you'd expect to lose 1.8%. On truly extreme days the average loss is 2.6%. For a £10,000 portfolio that's £180 and £260, in a single day.

Risk Metrics⏱ 3 min read

Max Drawdown

The worst peak-to-trough decline in your portfolio's history. This is the number that tests whether you would actually hold through a crash.

What It Measures

Max drawdown = (Peak value − Trough value) / Peak value. A drawdown of −30% means your portfolio fell 30% from its high before recovering. The S&P 500's max drawdown in 2008 was −57%; in the 2020 COVID crash it was −34%, but recovered in just five months.

Why It Matters

Many investors tolerate volatility on paper but panic-sell during a large drawdown, locking in permanent losses. If your strategy has a 40% max drawdown, ask honestly: could you hold through that without selling? This calibration is more important than any return figure.

Volatility

The standard deviation of returns, measuring how much your portfolio bounces around day to day. High volatility amplifies both gains and losses.

Annualised Volatility

Daily returns are small, so volatility is annualised by multiplying daily standard deviation by √252 (trading days per year). A portfolio with 1% daily standard deviation has annualised volatility of ~15.9%.

The VIX measures implied 30-day volatility of S&P 500 options, the "fear gauge." VIX > 30 signals market stress; < 15 signals calm. FlyingFunds uses historical (realised) volatility of your actual returns.

Is High Volatility Bad?

Not necessarily. Higher volatility often comes with higher expected return. For long-term investors with time to ride out dips, it can be appropriate. For those near retirement, lower volatility matters more. Match your portfolio volatility to your time horizon and psychological tolerance.

Efficient Markets Hypothesis

Can you consistently beat the market? The EMH says no, and the evidence largely agrees.

The Three Forms

  • Weak form: Prices reflect all historical price/volume data. Technical analysis cannot generate consistent alpha.
  • Semi-strong: Prices reflect all public information. Fundamental analysis can't consistently beat the market.
  • Strong form: Prices reflect all information including insider knowledge. Generally rejected. Insider trading is illegal precisely because it works.
What the Evidence Says

Evidence broadly supports weak and semi-strong efficiency for large-cap developed markets. ~80% of active fund managers underperform their benchmark net of fees over 10 years. Yet momentum, value, and size anomalies persist. Either markets aren't fully efficient, or these represent unrecognised risk premiums.

Active vs Passive Investing

One of the most important decisions any investor faces. The evidence may surprise you.

The Case for Passive

Passive investing means holding a diversified index fund and accepting the market return. Fees are minimal (0.05–0.20% p.a.) and infrequent trading reduces tax drag. Around 80% of active managers underperform their benchmark over 10 years net of fees.

The Case for Active

Active can outperform in specific circumstances: small-cap markets with less analyst coverage, emerging markets with less price efficiency, or strategies exploiting documented anomalies. Factor ETFs ("smart beta") allow semi-active exposure to momentum, value, and size effects at low cost.

The Fee Drag Problem

If a fund charges 1% p.a. and the market returns 7%, the manager needs 8% just to keep pace. Over 30 years, this compounding fee drag cuts your final wealth by ~26% versus a passive index fund. This maths is the core argument for passive investing.

Market Anomalies

If markets are efficient, why do momentum, value, and size effects persist? These documented anomalies have been exploited by quants for decades.

The Big Three Factors

  • Momentum: Stocks that outperformed over 3–12 months tend to continue outperforming. Jegadeesh & Titman (1993). Contradicts weak-form EMH.
  • Value (HML): High book-to-market stocks (cheap by accounting measures) historically outperform glamour stocks. Fama & French three-factor model (1993).
  • Size (SMB): Small-caps historically outperform large-caps on a risk-adjusted basis. Effect has weakened since discovery but persists in less-efficient markets.
Why Do They Persist?

Two competing explanations: (1) These are risk factors that standard models miss. Momentum crashes badly in market reversals. Investors are compensated for real risk. (2) Genuine inefficiencies caused by investor irrationality, but limits to arbitrage prevent full correction.

Behavioural Finance

Classical finance assumes rational investors. Behavioural finance proves they are not, and documents the systematic ways psychology drives markets away from efficiency.

Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)

People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, and they are loss-averse: losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. This explains holding losers too long, selling winners too early, and feeling worse about a 10% loss than happy about a 10% gain.

The Disposition Effect

Investors systematically sell winners early and hold losers, the opposite of "cut losses, let winners run." Realising a loss feels painful, so we hold the position hoping it recovers. The Investment Diary in FlyingFunds was designed to help you track this pattern in your own behaviour.

Why This Creates Anomalies

If investors make the same mistakes systematically, prices deviate from fundamental value in predictable ways. Limits to arbitrage (the costs and risks of correcting mispricings) allow these deviations to persist. This is why momentum and value effects survive even after becoming widely known.

Investor Biases

Loss aversion, overconfidence, anchoring and herding are the psychological traps that quietly derail well-intentioned portfolios.

The Five Most Costly Biases

  • Loss aversion: Losses feel ~2× as intense as equivalent gains. Causes the disposition effect, holding losers and selling winners.
  • Overconfidence: Most investors believe they are above-average. This leads to overtrading and underperformance via transaction costs and poor timing.
  • Anchoring: We fixate on an arbitrary reference (e.g. purchase price) and make decisions relative to it, not current fundamentals.
  • Herding: Following the crowd feels safe but leads to buying at peaks and selling at troughs, the opposite of optimal.
  • Recency bias: We overweight recent events, expecting bull markets to continue and fearing after crashes what has already happened.
Anchoring in Practice

An investor buys at £50. It falls to £30. Instead of assessing whether £30 is good value, they think "I need it to get back to £50." That £50 anchor distorts rational decision-making. The stock doesn't know what you paid for it.

Equities & How Stocks Work

Buying a share means buying a fractional ownership stake in a real company. Understanding what drives prices and how to read key metrics is the foundation of equity investing.

What You Actually Own

A share of stock gives you a proportional claim on a company's assets and future earnings. As a shareholder you may receive dividends when profits are distributed, and you can vote on major corporate decisions. If the company grows and generates more profit, the share price typically rises to reflect that increased value.

Equities sit at the top of the risk spectrum among mainstream asset classes. Shareholders are paid last if a company fails, after creditors and bondholders. That residual risk is why equities historically return more over long periods than bonds or cash.

What Drives the Share Price

  • Earnings: The most fundamental driver. When a company earns more than expected, the price usually rises.
  • Expectations: Markets are forward-looking. A stock can fall on good news if the news is not as good as the market anticipated.
  • Interest rates: Higher rates reduce the present value of future earnings and make bonds relatively more attractive, pushing equity prices down.
  • Sentiment and flows: Institutional buying, index rebalancing, and retail sentiment create short-term price movements that are disconnected from fundamentals.

Key Metrics

  • Earnings Per Share (EPS): Net profit divided by shares outstanding. Shows how profitable the company is per share owned.
  • Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio: Share price divided by EPS. A P/E of 20 means investors pay £20 for every £1 of annual earnings. High P/E suggests high growth expectations.
  • Market capitalisation: Share price multiplied by shares outstanding. The total market value of the company.
  • Dividend yield: Annual dividend per share divided by share price. A 3% yield means £3 income per year per £100 invested.
Reading the Numbers

A company with a share price of £50, EPS of £2.50, and annual dividend of £1.50 has: P/E of 20, and dividend yield of 3%. If the market expects earnings to grow at 15% per year, a P/E of 20 might be cheap. If growth is 2%, it might be expensive. Context always matters.

Where Stocks Trade

UK shares trade on the London Stock Exchange (LSE). US shares trade on the NYSE and NASDAQ. Many investors access global equities cheaply through index funds and ETFs, which removes the need to pick individual stocks entirely.

Reading Charts & Candlesticks

Candlestick charts are the most widely used way to visualise price action. Learning to read them reveals what buyers and sellers are doing, though the evidence on whether they predict the future is more mixed than most traders admit.

Anatomy of a Candlestick

Each candle represents one time period, showing four prices: Open, High, Low and Close (OHLC). The body is the rectangle between open and close. The thin lines extending above and below are called wicks or shadows, and they show the full range traded during the period.

  • Green (bullish) candle: Close is higher than open. Buyers were in control.
  • Red (bearish) candle: Close is lower than open. Sellers were in control.
  • Long body: Strong conviction in one direction.
  • Long wicks: Price moved sharply but was rejected, suggesting uncertainty or a potential reversal.
OHLC in Practice

A daily candle that opens at £100, reaches a high of £108, falls to a low of £97, and closes at £105 tells a clear story: buyers pushed the price up strongly, the bears managed to drag it back briefly, but buyers regained control by close. The long lower wick is often read as bullish.

Common Patterns

  • Doji: Open and close are nearly equal, creating a cross shape. Signals indecision. Context determines whether this is a reversal warning or just a pause.
  • Hammer: Small body at the top, long lower wick. Appears after a downtrend. Suggests sellers pushed prices lower but buyers rejected the move.
  • Shooting star: Small body at the bottom, long upper wick. Appears after an uptrend. Suggests buyers pushed prices higher but sellers rejected the move.
  • Bullish engulfing: A large green candle that fully covers the previous red candle. Often read as a reversal signal after a downtrend.
  • Bearish engulfing: A large red candle that fully covers the previous green candle. Often read as a reversal signal after an uptrend.

Support, Resistance and Trend Lines

Support is a price level where buying has historically emerged and prevented further declines. Resistance is a level where selling pressure has historically capped advances. Trend lines connect a series of highs or lows to show the direction of price movement.

These concepts are widely used but subjective. Two analysts looking at the same chart will often draw different lines. That subjectivity is worth keeping in mind before making trading decisions based on them.

Does Technical Analysis Actually Work?

The academic evidence is genuinely mixed. Some patterns, such as momentum, have robust empirical support. Others, such as specific candlestick formations, show much weaker predictive power once transaction costs are accounted for. The Efficient Market Hypothesis suggests most chartable patterns should be arbitraged away once known. In practice, technical analysis is most useful as a risk management framework, helping investors define clear entry and exit points rather than as a reliable crystal ball.

ETFs Explained

Exchange-traded funds give you instant diversification at very low cost. They have become the default choice for long-term investors for good reason, but not all ETFs are created equal.

What an ETF Is

An ETF is a basket of securities that trades on a stock exchange just like a single share. When you buy one unit of an S&P 500 ETF, you effectively own a tiny slice of all 500 companies in that index, weighted by market capitalisation. You can buy or sell throughout the trading day at market prices, unlike a traditional mutual fund, which is priced once daily.

Types of ETF

  • Index ETFs: Track a broad market index such as the FTSE 100, S&P 500 or MSCI World. These are the workhorses of passive investing.
  • Sector ETFs: Focus on a specific industry, such as technology, healthcare or clean energy. More concentrated, more volatile.
  • Factor ETFs (smart beta): Tilt toward proven risk factors such as value, momentum, quality or low volatility. Somewhere between pure passive and active management.
  • Bond ETFs: Hold a portfolio of government or corporate bonds. Useful for managing duration and credit exposure.
  • Commodity ETFs: Provide exposure to gold, oil or agricultural commodities, often through futures contracts rather than physical holdings.

Why Costs Matter More Than Almost Anything

The Total Expense Ratio (TER) is the annual fee deducted from fund assets. A passive global equity ETF typically charges 0.07–0.20% per year. An actively managed fund might charge 0.75–1.50%. Over 30 years, that 1% annual difference compounds into tens of thousands of pounds on a modest investment. The evidence consistently shows that most active funds fail to outperform their benchmark after fees over the long run, which is why costs are one of the few truly controllable variables in investing.

The Cost Compounding Effect

£10,000 invested for 30 years at 7% gross return: with a 0.15% TER, it grows to approximately £72,000. With a 1.15% TER, it grows to approximately £57,000. That 1% annual fee difference costs you around £15,000, purely from compounding friction.

Physical vs Synthetic ETFs

A physical ETF actually buys the underlying securities. A synthetic ETF uses derivatives (typically a swap with a bank) to replicate index returns without holding the shares directly. Synthetic ETFs can track illiquid or hard-to-access markets more efficiently, but they introduce counterparty risk. For core holdings, physical replication is generally preferred by most retail investors.

Crypto & Digital Assets

Crypto is genuinely novel and genuinely risky. Understanding what you are actually buying, why the volatility is so extreme, and what role it might play in a broader portfolio matters before you allocate anything.

What You Are Buying

Unlike shares, crypto gives you no ownership stake in a company, no claim on earnings, and no legal protections if things go wrong. What you own is a unit of a protocol or network. The value derives from belief in the utility of that network, scarcity, and the demand of other investors who want to own it.

  • Bitcoin (BTC): Designed as digital money with a fixed supply cap of 21 million coins. The scarcity is enforced by code, not a central bank. It is the most liquid and most widely held cryptocurrency.
  • Ethereum (ETH): A programmable blockchain that hosts decentralised applications, smart contracts and most of the DeFi and NFT ecosystem. Value is tied to demand for computation on its network.
  • Altcoins: Thousands of other cryptocurrencies exist, most with far less liquidity, weaker track records, and much higher risk of going to zero.

The Volatility Problem

Bitcoin has historically had annualised volatility of 60–100%, compared to roughly 15–20% for the S&P 500. A 50% decline in a year is a routine occurrence in crypto, not a tail event. That level of volatility has real consequences for portfolio construction. Even a 5% allocation to crypto can meaningfully shift the risk profile of an otherwise conservative portfolio.

Volatility in Practice

Bitcoin fell 80% from its November 2021 peak to its November 2022 low. An investor who held £10,000 worth at the peak was holding £2,000 twelve months later. It subsequently recovered and reached new highs, but not every investor had the stomach or the time horizon to hold through that decline.

The Unique Risks

  • Custody risk: If you hold crypto on an exchange and it fails or is hacked, you have no FSCS protection. Self-custody via a hardware wallet is safer but adds its own complexity and risk of loss.
  • Regulatory risk: Governments can restrict access, impose taxes, or change the legal status of crypto holdings. Regulatory landscape varies significantly across jurisdictions.
  • Altcoin and smart contract risk: Smaller tokens can lose all value overnight. Smart contract bugs have led to hundreds of millions of pounds being lost or stolen.
  • Liquidity risk: In a market panic, bid-ask spreads widen dramatically and exchanges can pause withdrawals, making exit at a reasonable price difficult.

Portfolio Role

Crypto has historically shown low correlation to equities over long periods, which is the theoretical case for including it. In practice, during stress events such as March 2020 and 2022, correlations with risk assets spiked toward 1, offering little diversification benefit exactly when you needed it most. Most institutional frameworks treat crypto as a speculative position, if included at all, capped at 1–5% of a portfolio and sized to be a position you could afford to lose entirely without it derailing your financial plan.
